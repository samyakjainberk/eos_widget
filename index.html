Progressive Sharpening & Edge of Stability

An interactive companion to Understanding Progressive Sharpening — full-batch gradient descent on a small network, with every panel tied to the paper's equations · build: theory-v5

Full-batch GD at rate $\eta$ on $f(x)\in\mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{out}}}$. Sharpness $\sigma_1=\lambda_{\max}(\nabla^2_\theta\mathcal L)$ rises (progressive sharpening) until it pins near $2/\eta$ at the edge of stability, where the loss oscillates with period 2.

Following Understanding Progressive Sharpening, over a window we freeze $J_{\theta_t}=\tfrac{df}{d\theta}$ and the function Hessian $Q_{\theta_t}=\tfrac{d^2f}{d\theta^2}$ ($Q_{\theta_{t+\Delta t}}=Q_{\theta_t}$) and predict from the Taylor surrogate

$$f_{\theta_{t+\Delta t}}=f_{\theta_t}+J_{\theta_t}^{\top}\Delta\theta+\Delta\theta^{\top}Q_{\theta_t}\Delta\theta .$$

Assumptions: (1) Gauss–Newton $G$ sets the top Hessian eigenvalues; (2) the surrogate holds for a few steps; (3) the residual $r=y-f$ (or its top-NTK projection) keeps its sign. For MSE, $\nabla^2_\theta\mathcal L=\underbrace{\tfrac1N\sum_i\nabla f_i\nabla f_i^{\top}}_{G}+\underbrace{\tfrac1N\sum_i(f_i-y_i)\nabla^2 f_i}_{S}$. Panels track $J$ on the eigenvectors of $H=\nabla^2_\theta\sum_i f$, the SLQ spectra, and how $H$'s eigenspaces rotate.

19 · gradient-norm change vs D²(NTK) — where each turns negative

Correlates two events over training. A = the one-step-ahead change in the gradient norm, $A_t=\lVert J_{t+1}^\top r_{t+1}\rVert-\lVert J_t^\top r_t\rVert$, where $r_{t+1}=(I-\tfrac{\eta}{N}\,\mathrm{NTK}_t)\,r_t$ and $J_{t+1}=J_t+\tfrac{\eta}{N}\,Q_t(J_t^\top r_t)$ are the first-order GD predictions ($Q_t$ = function Hessian, applied via a residual-weighted HVP; $\tfrac{\eta}{N}$ = the run's actual mean-loss GD step). B = $D^2(\mathrm{tr\,NTK})=\mathrm{tr\,NTK}_{t+1}-2\,\mathrm{tr\,NTK}_t+\mathrm{tr\,NTK}_{t-1}$, the centered 2nd difference of $\mathrm{tr\,NTK}=\lVert J\rVert_F^2$ (its "rate of change of rate of change"). The question: does the step where A first goes negative line up with the step where B first goes negative? Best with eigevery = 1 (consecutive GD steps); small N.

15 · 2nd-difference decomposition of $\lVert J\rVert_F^2$ and $\sigma_1$ over GD

Under gradient descent with step $\Delta\theta=\tfrac{\eta}{N}\sum_i r_i\nabla f_i$ (so $c=\eta/N$), the discrete second difference of $\lVert J\rVert_F^2=\mathrm{tr}(\mathrm{NTK})$ (Panel 1) and of $\sigma_1$ = the top NTK eigenvalue (Panel 2) is decomposed into the scalar theory terms (the prediction, Plot 1) + an $N\times N$ matrix $A$ (resp. $B$). What $A$/$B$ are: the matrix form of that same curvature — $A$ for $\lVert J\rVert^2_F$, $B$ for $\sigma_1$ — whose real eigenvalues (Plots 3–5) show how the predicted curvature is distributed across the $N$ sample modes (a few dominant directions vs. spread out; positive = sharpening, negative = flattening). Plot 5 is their full peak-normalized spectral density (height in $[0,1]$; the eigenvalue locations on the x-axis are the content). Each panel holds 3 consecutive eig-ticks $t,t{-}1,t{-}2$ (it skips its first two ticks) and evaluates the per-sample function-Hessian HVPs $Q_{t-1,k}\!\cdot,\,Q_{t-2,k}\!\cdot$. Single or multi-sample, small $N$ ($\le$ grid3dcap); assumes vanilla GD on mean-MSE; OFF by default. At $N=1$ the two panels coincide (the NTK is $1\times1$, so $\sigma_1=\lVert J\rVert^2$ and the $u_{t,1}$-projection is trivial). Requires eigevery=1 — the per-GD-step 2nd-difference needs three consecutive steps, so the panels stay empty at a larger eig-stride. The divergence% is an unbounded signed residual (it can go negative or exceed 100% — e.g. at the EoS sawtooth transitions where $D^2$ nearly vanishes), not a 0–100 fraction. It also grows $\propto$ lr: the terms capture only the $c^2=(\eta/N)^2$ (function-Hessian) order, whereas $D^2$ is the full discrete 2nd-difference, so the leftover is the $O(c^3)$ higher-order truncation — $\approx0$ at small lr, growing to tens of % at EoS-scale lr (verified to scale exactly $\propto$ lr on a quadratic model, where the dynamics are exact). This is the theory's truncation, not a numerical/code error. Shorthands: $g_s=\sum_i r_{s,i}\nabla f_{s,i}$, $S=\sum_k Q_{t-1,k}^2$, $\tilde Q=\sum_j r_{t-1,j}Q_{t-2,j}$.

Three phases of the projection onto the top-$n$ (1st of each pair) and bottom-$n$ (2nd) eigenvectors $u$ of $H=\sum_a Q_a=\sum_a\nabla^2 f_a$ (the summed function Hessian); $\sigma$ is the corresponding eigenvalue, divided by $N$ (per-sample scale, as in §2). Phase 1 · alignment: $|\langle J,u\rangle|/\lVert J\rVert=|\cos\angle(J,u)|$. Phase 2 · power iteration: $|\langle J,u\rangle|\,\sigma$ (eigenvalue-scaled), with $J=\sum_a\nabla f_a$ (summed gradient). Phase 3 · residual dominance: $|\langle J\!\cdot\!r,u\rangle|\,\sigma$ with $J$ contracted with the residual, $J\!\cdot\!r=\sum_a r_a\nabla f_a$ (cf. §4b, $r=y-f$); for a single sample the residual factors out and is kept signed — $r\,|\langle J,u\rangle|\,\sigma$.

4b · $J\!\cdot\!r$ projected onto eigenvectors of $Q[u_1]$ (NTK-weighted function Hessian)

Multi-sample only. $Q[u_1]=\sum_a (u_1)_a\,\nabla^2 f_a$ — the function Hessian weighted by the top NTK eigenvector $u_1$; $J\!\cdot\!r=\sum_a r_a\nabla f_a$ — the Jacobian contracted with the residual. We eigendecompose $Q[u_1]$ and project $J\!\cdot\!r$ onto its top-$n$ (1st) / bottom-$n$ (2nd) eigenvectors; panels 3–4 normalize by $\lVert J\!\cdot\!r\rVert$.

Bottom row: first two — the top-$n$ / bottom-$n$ projections of $J\!\cdot\!r$ divided by $\lVert r\rVert$ (norm of the $d_n$-dim residual). Last two — alignment $\langle g_1, w_k\rangle$ between the top Gauss–Newton eigenvector $g_1=J u_1/\lVert J u_1\rVert$ (SVD-paired with $u_1$) and the top-$n$ / bottom-$n$ eigenvectors $w_k$ of $Q[u_1]$.

4c · $Q[u_1]\,(J\!\cdot\!r)$ projected onto Gauss–Newton singular vectors

Multi-sample only. 4d · Per-group $J$ and $J\!\cdot\!r$ — samples split by initial residual sign

Multi-sample only.
